The Widget

What is the RPR Reports Widget?

The RPR Reports Widget is an embeddable tool that lets REALTORS® offer selected RPR reports directly from their own website or landing page.

You choose the reports. Your visitor chooses the area they care about. The widget collects their contact information, sends the lead where you want it to go and then gives the visitor access to the matching RPR report.

RPR provides the report and the local data. You bring the interpretation.

How It Works

How does the RPR Reports Widget work?

The widget works in two simple steps.

First, the visitor chooses an area from a dropdown and enters their contact information. Then, after submitting the form, they see a report card with a button to view the matching RPR report.

Between those two steps, the widget sends the lead to the destination you selected during setup.

The setup follows a simple path:

  1. Create dynamic RPR report links for the areas, properties or markets you want to feature.
  2. Add each report to the widget generator as a label and URL.
  3. Choose where you want leads to go.
  4. Customize the branding, form fields, display style and the text visitors see in the widget.
  5. Copy the embed code and place it on your website.
One widget, many reports. A single widget can include multiple report options — for example, reports for three ZIP codes, two neighborhoods or several local market areas, all from the same dropdown.

Your Leads

Where does the lead go?

You decide during setup.

The RPR Reports Widget does not simply email leads from RPR. Instead, you choose the destination that fits your follow-up process, such as a Google Sheet, Zapier, Slack, GoHighLevel, a phone push notification, a CRM workflow or another supported option.

Each lead includes the visitor’s contact information, the area they selected and the RPR report they requested.

That gives you more than a name and email address. It gives you the reason they engaged.

If someone requests a Market Trends Report for a specific ZIP code, your follow-up can start with that market. If someone requests a Trade Area Report, your follow-up can start with the location or business question they were researching.

The widget works best when the lead lands somewhere you already check.

The Difference

Why this is different from a standard contact form

A standard contact form usually starts with what the agent wants:

“Send me your information.”

The RPR Reports Widget starts with what the visitor wants:

“Choose the local report you want to see.”

That shift changes the value exchange.

Before the widget, a website visitor might browse a few pages and leave without telling you what they cared about. After the widget, that same visitor can request a report tied to a specific ZIP code, neighborhood, market area or trade area.

Now your follow-up has context. Instead of saying, “Thanks for visiting my website,” you can say:

I saw you requested the Market Trends Report for 94550. Inventory has shifted over the last few months, and I’d be happy to walk you through what that means if you are thinking about buying or selling.

That is more useful than a generic follow-up. It is also more natural.

The report gives you a reason to reconnect without forcing a hard sell.

The Reports

What kind of reports can you offer?

The widget is built around dynamic RPR report links. That means you create the report in RPR first, then add the shareable report link to the widget generator.

In practice, the widget can be used with any dynamic RPR report link that makes sense for your audience. It works especially well with reports people are likely to request from a website, such as Market Trends, Market Activity, Neighborhood, School and Trade Area reports.

Because the widget text is customizable, you can shape the experience around the reports you choose. That flexibility is what makes the widget useful across different pages, audiences and follow-up goals.

The Opportunity

The real opportunity is how you configure the reports

One of the biggest advantages of the RPR Reports Widget is that you are not limited to a single use case.

Because you create the report first in RPR, you decide what information to include, what information to hide and how the report supports the audience you are trying to reach. The same report type can serve very different purposes depending on how it is configured.

Think of the widget less as a report-delivery tool and more as a way to package local market information into something people actually want.

For Buyers

Weekend open house updates

A Market Activity Report can be configured to highlight open houses happening in a specific area.

Imagine promoting a “This Weekend’s Open Houses” report in your email newsletter every Friday. Visitors select their area, request the report and see upcoming open houses without having to search multiple websites.

You receive a lead from someone actively looking at homes.

For Sellers

Local market activity

That same Market Activity Report can be configured differently for homeowners.

Instead of focusing on open houses, you might highlight new listings, pending sales, price changes and recently sold properties in a ZIP code or neighborhood.

Now the report becomes a market pulse for homeowners who want to understand what is happening around them.

For Buyers Relocating

Compare a new area

Neighborhood Reports and School Reports can help buyers compare communities before they ever schedule a showing.

A visitor researching several areas can request reports for each location and begin narrowing their options while giving you valuable insight into where they are focusing their search.

For Investors & Commercial

Trade area research

Trade Area Reports can provide demographic, consumer-spending and business-location insights that support investment and site-selection decisions.

The report becomes a research tool, and the lead tells you which market area caught their attention.

Start with the audience, not the report

The most effective implementations usually begin with a simple question:

“What information would someone visiting this page actually want?”

Once you know the answer, you can build or customize an RPR report around that need, add it to the widget and create a stronger reason for someone to engage.

That is why there is not a single “best” way to use the RPR Reports Widget. The combination of report types, report settings, customizable widget text and local market knowledge creates dozens of possibilities.

The more intentionally you configure the report, the more useful the experience becomes for both the visitor and your follow-up.

Getting Started

Keep the first version simple

The widget can appear directly on a page, open from a floating button or launch from an existing button on your site.

You can also choose how much information to ask for. Minimal mode asks for area and email address. Full mode asks for first name, last name, email, phone and area.

For many agents, minimal mode is a good place to start because it keeps the request simple. The visitor gets the report they asked for, and you get enough information to follow up with context.

The text inside the widget is customizable, too. That means you can fine-tune the headline, subheadline, button text and other copy to match the reports you are offering.

Start with one report, one page and one lead destination. You can always expand from there.
